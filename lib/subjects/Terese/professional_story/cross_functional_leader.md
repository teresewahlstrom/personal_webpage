**Every technical organisation depends on the boundaries between its functions: engineering and production, sales and technical specialists, customers and developers. When those boundaries are well-managed, organisations move quickly. When they are not, the failures that accumulate there become the real bottlenecks.**

Engineering constraints, commercial commitments, and production realities speak different languages. Terese has spent her career working where those meet — not as a coordinator, but as someone who diagnoses why the handoff is failing and builds what is needed to make it work.

## Learning from organisational misalignment

Role after role, Terese noticed the same pattern: capable, well-intentioned people growing frustrated—pulled in different directions by different assumptions, terminology, priorities, and measures of success. Each perspective made sense in isolation. Together, they no longer aligned.

Sales assumed engineering had agreed. Engineering assumed sales understood the constraints. Different engineering disciplines struggled to reconcile problems that only emerged where their work came together. Production inherited decisions it had never been part of. Leadership often underestimated what organisational change required because they could not see the work beneath it.

The problem was rarely competence or commitment. It was that nobody owned the space between functions. Everyone owned their lane. Nobody owned what happened at the intersections—and the longer that disconnect went unaddressed, the more organisational damage it caused.

That insight reshaped how Terese approaches leadership. Rather than accepting the first definition of the problem, she first works to understand how each party sees it, then builds a shared understanding that everyone can align behind before designing a way forward.

Because she worked this way consistently, colleagues across teams sought her out when problems became difficult to untangle—not because they expected immediate answers, but because experience had taught them that, after talking it through, the real problem would often become clearer, making the right way forward much easier to see.

## In practice

* **Managing technical uncertainty under commercial pressure – GE Additive:** New machines and materials were still evolving while customers were evaluating major investments. Sales needed benchmark results to keep opportunities moving. Engineering could not yet promise performance that had not been fully validated. What was missing was not technical capability, but sufficient evidence. Technical uncertainty was being translated into commercial commitments before the necessary validation existed. Each benchmark programme became the point where commercial expectations, technical evidence, and customer decisions came together. Terese planned and delivered those programmes, working closely with R&D, operators, suppliers, and commercial teams to make technical constraints visible, establish realistic expectations, and give customers the evidence they needed to make informed investment decisions.

* **A shared operational baseline where none existed – Markforged:** The organisation believed it had a training problem. It actually had a shared-understanding problem. R&D taught one set of practices, support taught another, and operators had developed a third. Machine R&D, materials R&D, and leadership each had different views about what correct process and best practice actually looked like. The answer a customer received depended entirely on who they asked. Before training could improve, that fragmentation had to become visible. Once it did, Terese redesigned the boundary between those groups — developing a global programme that brought business leaders, PhD-level specialists, and operators into a common understanding of how the technology worked and how it should be taught. Customers stopped receiving different answers. As the shared baseline took hold, she transferred teaching responsibilities to operators, allowing R&D to spend more time on development rather than teaching.

* **Making organisational value visible – Markforged:** When a new internal laboratory was established, the team's responsibilities expanded rapidly, but leadership's understanding of what the team now contributed did not keep pace. Having seen Terese help other parts of the organisation articulate complex technical work and capability, colleagues from the laboratory approached her for help. Together they captured the capabilities the team had built, the responsibilities they had assumed, and the operational value they now delivered—closing the gap between what leadership believed the team did and what it had actually become.

* **Shaping future capability through structured analysis – Volvo Cars:** As part of the company's strategic additive manufacturing hub, Terese helped evaluate how the organisation should develop its additive manufacturing capability. Many initiatives were still exploratory, requiring technical analysis before they could become actionable. Working across engineering disciplines, she identified the knowledge, tools, experiments, resources, and implementation effort different directions would require, translating speculative ideas into structured decision material for leadership. By clarifying both the practical implications and the strategic value of additive manufacturing, she helped leadership compare alternatives, understand where investment would have the greatest impact, and make more informed decisions about future capability development.
