**Terese leads across technical, commercial, and operational boundaries. She helps people with different expertise, priorities, and constraints develop a shared understanding of complex problems, enabling organizations to make better decisions and execute with greater confidence.**

Every technical organization depends on the boundaries between its functions: engineering and production, sales and technical specialists, customers and developers. When those boundaries are well-managed, organizations move quickly. When they are not, the failures that accumulate there become the real bottlenecks.

## Learning from organizational misalignment

Across her career, the same pattern kept resurfacing: capable, well-intentioned people growing frustrated—pulled in different directions by different assumptions, terminology, priorities, and measures of success. Each perspective made sense in isolation. Together, they no longer aligned.

Sales assumed engineering had agreed. Engineering assumed sales understood the constraints. Different engineering disciplines struggled to reconcile problems that only emerged where their work came together. Production inherited decisions it had never been part of. Leadership often underestimated what organizational change required because they could not see the work beneath it.

Nobody owned the space between functions. Everyone owned their lane. Nobody owned what happened at the intersections—and the longer that disconnect went unaddressed, the more organizational damage it caused.

That realization shaped how Terese approaches leadership. Rather than solving the problem immediately, she first works to understand how each group sees the situation, where their assumptions differ, and which constraints each perspective captures. By making differing assumptions and hidden dependencies visible, discussions that initially appear to be technical disagreements often become opportunities to build a shared understanding. People working with her often experience the result as greater clarity rather than stronger direction. As the problem becomes clearer, better decisions become easier to reach, and people move forward understanding not just what was decided, but why.

## Cross-functional leadership in practice

* **Leading customer benchmark programs – GE Additive:** Customers, sales, operators, suppliers, and R&D often held different—sometimes incompatible—views of what a benchmark needed to prove before a major investment decision could be made. Sales wanted commercial momentum. R&D wanted technical rigor. Customers wanted certainty. Left unaligned, each group would interpret the same results differently or disagree about what the benchmark had even tested.

  Terese led benchmark programs that functioned less as technical tests and more as shared artifacts everyone could stand behind. By bringing customers, sales, operators, suppliers, and R&D into the same process—agreeing in advance what would be tested, how, and what would count as success—the benchmark became the point of reference rather than competing internal narratives. The manufactured parts and production-economics estimates mattered, but the deeper value was that everyone left the program working from the same understanding of what was true.

  For emerging machines and materials, she worked closely with R&D to adapt benchmark activities as both the technology and process understanding evolved, while maintaining that shared understanding across functions with very different incentives. Throughout, she made technical constraints visible, set realistic expectations, and enabled commercial teams to move opportunities forward with confidence grounded in evidence the whole organization understood.

* **Building a shared operational baseline – Markforged:** The organization believed it had a training problem. In reality, it had a shared-understanding problem. Machine R&D, Materials R&D, operators, support, and customers each held different views of how the technology worked and what constituted best practice. The answer a customer received depended entirely on whom they asked—not because any one group was wrong, but because each had converged on its own internal version of the truth, optimized for its own priorities and incentives.

  Terese first made that fragmentation visible, then synthesized knowledge across engineers and operators to establish a shared operational baseline—a common language for how the technology functioned and how it should be used. She rebuilt the global training approach around that baseline, developing reusable guidance, growing operators into confident trainers, and creating clearer boundaries between routine instruction and expert escalation. As capability spread through the organization, specialists spent less time repeatedly explaining fundamentals and more time advancing the technology itself.

  Customers stopped receiving conflicting guidance depending on who they reached. Commercial teams could communicate with greater confidence because they were no longer guessing which internal version of the truth was current. People weren't just better trained; they developed the confidence to teach others, answer recurring questions independently, and build on a common understanding rather than relying on whichever expert happened to be available.

* **Making organizational work visible – Markforged and GE Additive:** As teams evolved, their responsibilities often expanded faster than they were formally recognized or clearly described. Terese repeatedly helped teams articulate what they actually did, the responsibilities they had assumed, how their work connected to the wider organization, and why it mattered. By making implicit responsibilities explicit, she helped leadership make better-informed decisions about ownership, resource allocation, priorities, and future development while giving teams a clearer understanding of the value they created and the role they played within the wider organization.

* **Shaping future capability through structured analysis – Volvo Cars:** As part of the company's strategic manufacturing-technology hub, Terese helped evaluate how the organization should develop future capability. Many initiatives were still exploratory, requiring technical analysis before they could become actionable. Working across engineering, manufacturing, and leadership, she identified the knowledge, tools, experiments, resources, and implementation effort different directions would require, translating speculative ideas into structured decision material for leadership. By clarifying both the practical implications and the strategic value of the technology, she helped leadership compare alternatives, understand where investment would have the greatest impact, and make more informed decisions about future capability development.
