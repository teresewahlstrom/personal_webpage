**Every technical organisation depends on the boundaries between its functions: engineering and production, sales and technical specialists, customers and developers. When those boundaries are well-managed, organisations move quickly. When they are not, the failures that accumulate there become the real bottlenecks.**

Engineering drawings, commercial contracts, and factory schedules speak different languages. Terese has spent her career working at the seams where they meet — not as a coordinator, but as someone who diagnoses why the handoff is failing and builds something that makes it work.

## Learning from organisational misalignment

Early in her career, Terese repeatedly found herself at the same juncture across very different settings — between commercial and engineering teams, between R&D disciplines, between production and development. Each function had optimised for its own reality. Each believed the others understood their constraints. They did not.

Sales assumed engineering had agreed. Engineering assumed sales understood the limitations. Operations inherited the consequences. No single function had acted in bad faith. The misalignment had grown quietly through gaps that nobody had been asked to manage.

That was the insight that took longest to articulate: the problem was rarely that people lacked competence or commitment. It was that nobody owned the gap itself — the point where responsibilities, information, and unspoken assumptions crossed organisational boundaries. Everyone owned their function. Nobody owned what happened between them.

Those experiences shaped how she works. Her first instinct is rarely to solve the problem. It is to understand what problem each person believes they are trying to solve. That process often reveals that different parts of the organisation are trying to solve different problems altogether — and that the real work is making that visible before anyone commits to a solution. Only once the assumptions, constraints, responsibilities, and missing context are on the table does she begin designing a way forward.

Because she consistently approached problems this way, colleagues across organisations and reporting lines increasingly sought her out. People rarely came because they expected her to own the problem. They came because experience had taught them that, after talking it through with her, the situation would usually become clearer and the next step easier to see.

## In practice

* **Machine launches under conflicting commercial and technical constraints — GE Additive:** Customers were waiting for delivery dates. Engineering could not yet promise the performance. Production needed to know what to prepare for. Each function needed answers from the others before it could move — and the commitments already made to customers did not leave room for that uncertainty to play out quietly. Throughout machine launches, material introductions, benchmark programmes, and major demonstration projects, Terese worked at the point where those pressures met — coordinating R&D, operators, suppliers, and commercial stakeholders to make the constraints on each side visible, and to keep programmes moving despite uncertainty that ran through the entire delivery chain.

* **A shared operational baseline where none existed — Markforged:** The stated problem was that training needed to improve. The real problem, which nobody had yet seen, was that every part of the organisation held a different version of the truth. R&D taught one set of practices, support taught another, and operators had developed a third. Machine R&D, materials R&D, and leadership each had different views about what correct process and best practice actually looked like. The answer a customer received depended entirely on who they asked. Before training could improve, that fragmentation had to become visible. Once it did, Terese redesigned the boundary between those groups — developing a global programme that brought business leaders, PhD-level specialists, and operators into a common understanding of how the technology worked and how it should be taught. Customers stopped receiving different answers. As the shared baseline took hold, she transferred teaching responsibilities to internal teams, reducing dependence on a small number of experts.

* **Making invisible contribution legible — cross-organisational:** Colleagues from a separate part of the organisation approached Terese for help, despite her having no reporting relationship or formal role in their function. They had established a new internal laboratory and taken on significantly expanded responsibilities, but could not explain what had changed or why it mattered. The gap between the work being performed and the organisation's understanding of it had become the problem. Terese helped them reconstruct their contribution — the capabilities developed, the responsibilities assumed, the operational value now delivered — and translate it into something leadership could evaluate on evidence rather than impression.
