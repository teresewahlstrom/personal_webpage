**Terese leads across technical, commercial, and operational boundaries. She helps people with different expertise, priorities, and constraints align around a shared understanding of complex problems, enabling organizations to make better decisions and execute with greater confidence.**

Every technical organization depends on the boundaries between its functions: engineering and production, sales and technical specialists, customers and developers. When those boundaries are well-managed, organizations move quickly. When they are not, the failures that accumulate there become the real bottlenecks.

## Learning from organizational misalignment

Across her career, the same pattern kept resurfacing: capable, well-intentioned people growing frustrated—pulled in different directions by different assumptions, terminology, priorities, and measures of success. Each perspective made sense in isolation. Together, they no longer aligned.

Sales assumed engineering had agreed. Engineering assumed sales understood the constraints. Different engineering disciplines struggled to reconcile problems that only emerged where their work came together. Production inherited decisions it had never been part of. Leadership often underestimated what organizational change required because they could not see the work beneath it.

Nobody owned the space between functions. Everyone owned their lane. Nobody owned what happened at the intersections—and the longer that disconnect went unaddressed, the more organizational damage it caused.

## Cross-functional leadership in practice

* **Leading customer benchmark programs – GE Additive:** Customers, sales, operators, suppliers, and R&D often held different — sometimes incompatible — views of what a benchmark needed to prove before a major investment decision could be made. Sales wanted commercial momentum. R&D wanted technical rigor. Customers wanted certainty. Left unaligned, each group would interpret the same results differently, or disagree about what the benchmark had even tested.

  Terese led benchmark programs that functioned less as a technical test and more as a shared artifact everyone could stand behind. By bringing customers, sales, operators, suppliers, and R&D into the same process — agreeing in advance what would be tested, how, and what would count as success — the benchmark became the thing all parties pointed to, rather than competing internal narratives. The manufactured parts and production-economics estimates mattered, but the deeper value was that everyone left the program working from the same understanding of what was true.

  For emerging machines and materials, she worked closely with R&D to adapt benchmark activities as the technology and process understanding evolved, while keeping that shared understanding intact across functions with very different incentives. Throughout, she made technical constraints visible, set realistic expectations, and enabled commercial teams to move opportunities forward with confidence grounded in something the whole organization agreed on.

* **A shared operational baseline where none existed – Markforged:** The organization believed it had a training problem. In reality, it had a shared-understanding problem. Machine R&D, Materials R&D, operators, support, and customers each held different views of how the technology worked and what constituted best practice. The answer a customer received depended entirely on whom they asked — not because any one group was wrong, but because each had converged on its own internal version of the truth, optimized for its own priorities and incentives.

  Terese first made that fragmentation visible, then synthesized knowledge across engineers and operators to establish a shared operational baseline — a common language for how the technology functioned and how it should be used. Once that common understanding existed, the interfaces between groups stopped being places where inconsistent answers leaked through. Customers stopped receiving conflicting guidance depending on who they reached. Commercial teams could speak with confidence because they were no longer guessing which internal version of the truth was current. People weren't just better trained; they had stopped teaching different versions of the same technology.

* **Making organizational work visible – Markforged and GE Additive:** As teams evolved, their responsibilities often expanded faster than they were formally recognized or clearly described. Terese repeatedly helped teams articulate what they actually did, the responsibilities they had assumed, how their work connected to the wider organization, and why it mattered. By making implicit responsibilities explicit, she helped leadership make better-informed decisions about ownership, resource allocation, priorities, and future development.

* **Shaping future capability through structured analysis – Volvo Cars:** As part of the company's strategic manufacturing-technology hub, Terese helped evaluate how the organization should develop future capability. Many initiatives were still exploratory, requiring technical analysis before they could become actionable. Working across engineering, manufacturing, and leadership, she identified the knowledge, tools, experiments, resources, and implementation effort different directions would require, translating speculative ideas into structured decision material for leadership. By clarifying both the practical implications and the strategic value of the technology, she helped leadership compare alternatives, understand where investment would have the greatest impact, and make more informed decisions about future capability development.
