**Terese industrializes metal Additive Manufacturing (AM), the industrial form of what is commonly known as 3D printing. She has worked across the entire industrial value chain—evaluating technologies as an industrial adopter, helping develop and industrialize them from within machine manufacturers, and relying on them in certified production. This breadth of experience gives her a practical understanding of what it takes to move metal AM from proof of concept to dependable industrial capability.**

AM is one of Terese's deepest specialist domains and one of the strongest demonstrations of her broader capabilities in product R&D, computational engineering, cross-functional leadership, and capability architecture. Building on the research presented in her widely read [Master's thesis](https://lup.lub.lu.se/student-papers/search/publication/8868478) (with over 6,500 downloads across 50+ countries), her expertise has since been refined through nearly a decade of industrial practice across the metal AM value chain.

A consistent question has run through that career: *Is this application a good fit for AM? Which technology is most appropriate? Is it technically and economically viable? And can it be scaled into reliable production?*

At Volvo Cars, she evaluated AM from the perspective of an industrial adopter—investing in AM equipment and sourcing printed components. At GE Additive and Markforged, she worked from within machine manufacturers that not only developed and sold AM systems, but also operated them to validate, externally demonstrate, and industrialize the technology in real production environments—together giving her an end-to-end understanding of the technical, commercial, and operational realities of reliable industrial metal AM.

Across these roles, her focus has been remarkably consistent: *understanding what has to become true before metal AM can move from technical potential to reliable industrial adoption.* Whether evaluating technologies, qualifying applications, stabilizing production, or embedding expertise into everyday work, Terese helps organizations make metal AM commercially and operationally viable.

## Metal AM in practice

* **Benchmark programs as qualification – GE Additive:** Establishing whether an application was industrially viable required answering a consistent sequence of questions: *Can this geometry be manufactured? Can the required mechanical properties be achieved? Within cost and lead-time constraints? Repeatably at production scale?* Terese ran benchmark programs as a qualification protocol against that sequence, manufacturing representative parts and identifying where the process, geometry, or requirement itself needed to change before the application was industrially ready. The output was not a manufactured sample, but a qualified answer on fit for production.

* **Navigating AM-specific economics – Across Volvo Cars, GE Additive, and Markforged:** Unlike many conventional manufacturing processes, AM economics depend on strongly interacting technical factors: machine utilization, build composition, support strategy, powder reuse, post-processing, and qualification requirements. Small technical decisions here can shift a part from commercially viable to unworkable. Terese helped organizations navigate those relationships, combining engineering analysis with production economics so investment decisions reflected how industrial AM actually behaves rather than simplified cost assumptions carried over from conventional manufacturing.

* **Design for Additive Manufacturing (DfAM) and process adaptation – Across Volvo Cars, GE Additive, and Markforged:** Successful AM quite often depends on adapting either the manufacturing process, the product design, or even the underlying requirements. Rather than treating an existing design as fixed, Terese evaluates where changes can improve manufacturability, cost, quality, or production robustness while respecting the scope of change that each project allows.

  Her work commonly operates at three complementary levels:

  * **Adapt process** – Optimizing parameters, build preparation, and post-processing to manufacture existing designs more reliably.

  * **Adapt geometry** – From minor geometric adjustments to complete redesign of individual components or entire assemblies to better exploit AM.

  * **Challenge requirements** – Distinguishing essential requirements from inherited assumptions or unnecessary constraints to identify simpler, more manufacturable solutions without compromising function.

  An important enabler is Terese's advanced geometric modelling capability across parametric solids, surface modelling, meshes, Sub-D, scan data, and geometry scripting — letting her exploit AM's design freedom where it creates engineering value, while remaining equally comfortable with simpler, more conventional solutions where they're the better choice.

* **Production systems execution – Markforged and GE Additive:** She worked across the complete AM workflow required to turn qualified applications into manufactured parts—from manufacturability review and build preparation through production planning, scheduling, quality control, process handoffs, and preserving process knowledge across iterations. Experience from both ISO 9001 production at Markforged and benchmark manufacturing at GE Additive gave first-hand insight into how engineering, operations, quality systems, and traceable records work together for repeatable industrial execution.

* **Process analysis and troubleshooting – Markforged:** She introduced structured logging, operational baselines, statistical process monitoring, and systematic defect analysis to detect process drift earlier and improve troubleshooting. By connecting build histories and process data with physical defect morphology, she helped establish clearer diagnostic logic, enabling faster root-cause analysis and more effective escalation.

* **Industrialization beyond the machine – Markforged:** Successfully industrializing metal AM requires more than capable machines, qualified materials, and validated processes. It also requires the knowledge needed to operate it consistently to become part of everyday work. Terese turned specialized manufacturing know-how—including process behavior, troubleshooting logic, defect interpretation, production best practices, and documentation—into practical guidance that operators and engineers used every day.

  Production became more consistent not because the technology changed, but because the knowledge required to run it became part of the production system itself. This reflects a recurring theme throughout Terese's career: moving metal AM from technical potential to repeatable industrial capability by embedding manufacturing expertise into everyday engineering and production systems.
