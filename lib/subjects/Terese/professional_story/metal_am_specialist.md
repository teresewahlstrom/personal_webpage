**Terese industrializes metal Additive Manufacturing (AM, also known as 3D printing). She has worked across the entire industrial value chain—evaluating technologies as an industrial adopter, helping develop and industrialize them from within machine manufacturers, and relying on them in certified production. This breadth of experience gives her a practical understanding of what it takes to move metal AM from proof of concept to dependable industrial capability.**

Metal AM is one of Terese's deepest specialist domains and one of the strongest demonstrations of her broader capabilities in product R&D, computational engineering, cross-functional leadership, and capability architecture. Built on the foundation of her widely read [Master's thesis](https://lup.lub.lu.se/student-papers/search/publication/8868478) (with over 6,500 downloads across 50+ countries), her expertise has since been refined through nearly a decade of industrial practice across the Additive Manufacturing value chain.

A consistent question has run through that career: *Is this application a good fit for AM? Which technology is most appropriate? Is it technically and economically viable? And can it be scaled into reliable production?*

At Volvo Cars, she evaluated AM from the perspective of an industrial adopter. At GE Additive and Markforged, she worked from within machine manufacturers that both advanced the technology and operated it to produce customer parts and production components.

Together, these experiences provided an end-to-end understanding of the technical, commercial, and operational realities required to establish reliable industrial metal AM.

Across these roles, her focus has been remarkably consistent: understanding what has to become true before metal AM can move from technical potential to reliable industrial adoption. Whether evaluating technologies, qualifying applications, stabilizing production, or embedding expertise into everyday work, Terese helps organizations make metal AM commercially and operationally viable.

## Metal AM in practice

* **Strategic technology evaluation – Volvo Cars:** Her work included technology scouting, comparative proof-of-concept evaluation across AM technologies, production-suitability assessment, and make-versus-buy decision support. She assessed where AM met application-specific technical requirements and where conventional manufacturing remained the better choice, balancing technical capability with lifecycle cost, environmental impact, and safety considerations. This enabled better technology selection, stronger business cases, and earlier rejection of poor-fit applications.

* **Benchmark programs as qualification – GE Additive:** Establishing whether an application was industrially viable for metal AM required answering a specific sequence of questions: *Can this geometry actually be manufactured? Can the required mechanical properties be achieved? Is the necessary post-processing acceptable within cost and lead-time constraints? Is the process repeatable at production scale?* Terese ran benchmark programs as a qualification protocol against that sequence — manufacturing representative parts, evaluating them against the application's real requirements, and identifying where the process, the geometry, or the requirement itself needed to change before the application could be considered industrially ready. The output was not just a manufactured sample, but a qualified answer to whether the technology was actually fit for that production use.

* **Navigating AM-specific economics – Across Volvo Cars, GE Additive, and Markforged:** Unlike many conventional manufacturing processes, additive manufacturing economics depend on strongly interacting technical factors: machine utilization, build composition, support strategy, powder reuse, post-processing, and qualification requirements. Small technical decisions can therefore change commercial viability dramatically — a build orientation, a support strategy, or a powder-reuse assumption can shift a part from commercially viable to unworkable. Terese helped organizations navigate those relationships, combining engineering analysis with production economics so investment decisions reflected how industrial AM actually behaves rather than simplified cost assumptions carried over from conventional manufacturing.

* **Design for Additive Manufacturing (DfAM) and process adaptation – Across Volvo Cars, GE Additive, and Markforged:** Successful AM often depends on adapting either the manufacturing process, the product design, or even the underlying requirements. Rather than treating an existing design as fixed, Terese evaluates where changes can improve manufacturability, cost, quality, or production robustness while respecting what types of adaptations that the customer allows.

  Her work commonly operates at three complementary levels:

  * **Adapting process** – Optimizing build preparation, process parameters, support strategies, orientation, and post-processing to manufacture existing designs more reliably. Where appropriate, she also documents the resulting process to improve repeatability and future production.

  * **Adapting geometry** – Even relatively small design changes can significantly improve manufacturability. This may range from minor geometric adjustments to complete redesign of individual components or even entire assemblies to better exploit additive manufacturing.

  * **Challenge requirements** – Engineering drawings and specifications do not always reflect true functional requirements. By distinguishing essential requirements from inherited assumptions or unnecessary constraints, she has often identified simpler and more manufacturable solutions without compromising the product's intended function.

  An important enabler of this work is Terese's advanced geometric modelling capability. She is comfortable working across parametric solids, surface modelling, meshes, Sub-D, scan data, and geometry scripting. This breadth allows her to exploit Additive Manufacturing's design freedom where it creates engineering value, while remaining equally comfortable with simpler, more conventional solutions.

* **Production systems execution – Markforged and GE Additive:** She worked across the complete AM workflow required to turn qualified applications into manufactured parts—from manufacturability review and build preparation through production planning, scheduling, quality control, and process handoffs. Experience from both ISO 9001 production at Markforged and benchmark manufacturing at GE Additive provided first-hand insight into how engineering, operations, and quality systems must work together to achieve repeatable industrial execution.

* **Process analysis and troubleshooting – Markforged:** She introduced structured logging, operational baselines, statistical process monitoring, and systematic defect analysis to detect process drift earlier and improve troubleshooting. By connecting build histories and process data with physical defect morphology, she helped establish clearer diagnostic logic, enabling faster root-cause analysis and more effective escalation.

* **Industrialization beyond the machine – Markforged:** Reliable industrial metal AM is often assumed to be a question of machines and materials. In practice, even well-understood technology fails to produce consistent results if the knowledge needed to run it well lives only with a handful of specialists rather than in everyday operation. Terese turned specialized manufacturing know-how — process behavior, troubleshooting logic, defect interpretation — into operational guidance that operators and engineers used routinely, not as reference material consulted occasionally. Production became more consistent not because the technology changed, but because the production knowledge required to run it had become part of how the line actually operated, rather than knowledge held in reserve by a few experts.

  This reflects a recurring theme throughout her career: helping organizations move metal additive manufacturing from technical potential to repeatable industrial capability by embedding manufacturing expertise into the systems, processes, and knowledge that support everyday engineering and production.
