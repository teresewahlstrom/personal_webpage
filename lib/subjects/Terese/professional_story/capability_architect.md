Terese treats organisational capability like an engineering problem: understanding how work actually gets done, identifying where execution depends on individuals rather than systems, and designing the structures needed for consistent performance at scale. Her work reduces organisational fragility by making critical knowledge, decisions, and workflows less dependent on specific individuals and more supported by repeatable, transferable systems.

Throughout her career, Terese repeatedly encountered the same patterns: recurring questions, inconsistent decisions, unclear handoffs, troubleshooting dead ends, and manual workarounds. Rather than treating these as isolated problems, she learned to view them as design signals. They often indicate deeper capability gaps: work depends on individual experience, critical decisions lack structure, responsibilities are unclear, or important knowledge and processes have not been translated into repeatable organisational systems.

Terese does not simply capture existing knowledge. She combines expertise from multiple stakeholders with her own engineering judgement, analysis, and systems thinking to identify what is missing, challenge assumptions, and design better ways of working. The solution is not always a knowledge resource. Depending on the problem, it may be a workflow, decision framework, diagnostic tool, training architecture, escalation model, feedback mechanism, or shared reference system. The objective is reliable organisational capability, not documentation.

Her approach follows a consistent pattern: **Diagnose** - **Design** - **Enable**

* **Diagnose:** Identify where execution breaks down. Understand the dependencies, decisions, bottlenecks, and failure modes that create fragility.
* **Design:** Create the processes, decision frameworks, tools, models, ownership structures, and knowledge resources the situation requires — including elements that do not yet exist.
* **Enable:** Embed those structures into how people actually work, so improvements become repeatable and do not depend on her continued involvement.

## Capability architecture in practice

* **Shared diagnostic capability - Markforged:** She established a physical defect-reference table as a shared organisational resource. Scrap parts were organised by defect morphology and likely process stage defect occurred, with each sample connected to the observations, discussions, and lessons surrounding it. The collection became a common reference point used across technical, operational, commercial, and leadership functions, as well as in customer-facing discussions. Over time, others began contributing additional defects and observations as well, allowing lessons that would normally remain isolated within individual troubleshooting cases to become part of a growing shared reference collection. By giving teams a common point of reference, the table made it easier to compare observations, discuss possible root causes, explain process limitations, and learn from previous investigations rather than starting from scratch each time.
* **Decision frameworks - Volvo Cars, GE Additive, and Markforged:** After repeatedly seeing important decisions depend on fragmented specialist reasoning, she built structured Excel-based models, templates, and workflow frameworks that made the governing logic more explicit and reusable. These captured sourcing trade-offs, configuration logic, production-cycle behaviour, equipment constraints, consumable demand, application requirements, and recurring support decisions. This enabled sales, support, and engineering teams, as well as customers, to evaluate options more independently, reducing reliance on scarce domain experts for recurring questions.
* **Technical enablement architecture - Markforged:** She replaced a fragmented customer-training setup with a structured global enablement system. Previously, onboarding was not consistently aligned with Machine R&D and Materials R&D best practices, important knowledge remained scattered across experts, and specialists were repeatedly drawn into avoidable customer support. She brought the relevant expertise together, created a deliberate training architecture, authored about 30 reusable guides, and established clearer boundaries between first-line support and expert escalation. The resulting system supported over 100 participants globally, gave customers more consistent guidance, allowed operators to take on a larger share of training responsibilities, and reduced the number of questions that required specialist involvement.
