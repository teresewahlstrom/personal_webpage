## Capability Architect

Terese’s belief in capability architecture was forged in environments where clarity had to be designed into the system for capability to scale. These were settings where technical dependencies ran deep, operational conditions evolved quickly, and small misalignments could cascade into costly consequences.

Working at that frontier revealed a recurring pattern: when an organization lacks a structured framework for navigating complexity, execution becomes fragile. Critical knowledge is often either trapped in individual expertise or hidden within complex systems themselves, requiring deliberate investigation to surface. Without those structures, context fragments, hidden variables accumulate, and learning becomes difficult to retain, synthesize, and scale.

Those patterns became clearer through exposure to radically different capability environments: from mature systems to barely codified ones, from democratized knowledge cultures to fragmented or tightly guarded expertise, and across settings spanning R&D, operations, commercial teams, global enterprises, and smaller organizations. Across those contrasts, the same principle emerged: capability scales only when critical knowledge becomes transferable, aligned, and structurally accessible.

From that reality emerged a defining conviction: capability must be architected. Complex organizations inevitably develop invisible fractures between functions, perspectives, and sources of truth. Engineering speaks one language. Operations another. Leadership sees metrics while execution reveals what the numbers cannot. Valuable knowledge becomes fragmented across teams, tools, and individuals.

For Terese, capability architecture is the translation of fragile, experience-driven insight into the structures that make complex technical reality usable beyond the individuals who first uncovered it.

The objective is always the same: to make complex technical reality stable enough to scale, clear enough to trust, and structured enough to execute.

Through this work, Terese learned to surface hidden signals within complex systems and translate them into frameworks that allowed people with entirely different responsibilities to operate around the same technical reality: cost models, configuration models, diagnostic systems, training architectures, operational feedback loops, and decision-support structures.

One concrete expression of this was Terese’s initiative to create a curated defect table, organized by failure mechanism and connected to engineering observations and troubleshooting context. It gave operators and engineers a shared physical reference for collective reasoning, transforming scattered operational observations into distributed diagnostic capability. Over time, others began contributing their own failed parts to the collection, turning it into a shared focal point for diagnosis and continuous learning.

## Cross-Functional Leader
Terese’s work has consistently operated between functions rather than inside a single silo. Across automotive departments, industrial metal additive manufacturing, customer enablement, and software-supported workflow systems, she repeatedly worked in the gaps between engineering, operations, support, sales, product management, suppliers, and customers: environments where no single group fully owned the whole problem and where alignment itself became part of execution.

Her leadership style centers on finding the real constraint, building shared understanding across functions, translating ambiguity into operating logic, and helping teams execute with greater autonomy and confidence. It operates through systems influence rather than org-chart authority: **creating the structures, conversations, tools, and decision logic that allow people with different responsibilities to move around the same technical reality**. She creates translation layers between complex technical systems (materials, machine, software pipelines, and data) and real-world organizational execution.

**Trust-Based Translation**
Her cross-functional leadership is not only structural; it is relational. She builds trust with operators, experts, customers, and internal stakeholders, then translates their partial perspectives into shared understanding and coordinated action. In training and customer-facing settings, she has led multi-expert sessions across operators, material R&D, machine R&D, and build preparation, while coaching contributors on how to communicate complex technical knowledge in ways customers could actually use.

**System Integrity Investigation**
Terese’s investigative approach focuses on how technical intent survives across engineering systems, software layers, operational constraints, and customer deployment. She identifies where information degrades, assumptions diverge, or system logic breaks down before those failures become organizational friction.

**Alignment Under Fragmentation**
She does not treat organizational problems as purely "people problems" or purely "technical bugs." She operates at the intersection of both. When different groups each hold different puzzle pieces of truth, she creates shared structures: technical references, cost models, training systems, diagnostic frameworks, and execution plans that make cross-functional decisions easier to trust.

**Rescue Execution**
Under pressure, her leadership becomes practical orchestration. At GE Additive, that meant coordinating operators, material R&D, suppliers, logistics, external finishing partners, and customer commitments under deadline pressure, including milestone-critical benchmark and launch-demo situations. At Markforged, it meant coordinating operators, material R&D, machine R&D, build preparation, support, and customer-facing training around live binder-jetting production.

> **The Bottom Line:** Terese builds execution capacity where technical complexity, product strategy, and distributed knowledge intersect. Her work focuses on turning cross-domain ambiguity into coherent logic, scalable organizational infrastructure, and more reliable cross-team decision-making.

## Deep into Industrial Metal AM
**Metal additive manufacturing is frontier systems engineering under incomplete rules and high technical coupling.** It is far from click-and-play. People who succeed in this field operate in a newer, less codified environment where the rulebook is still being written. Terese has worked at the source of that reality across two OEMs, and from the outside looking in.

Terese's additive-manufacturing experience spans the path from technology evaluation to live industrial execution. Across automotive, aerospace, medical, and industrial applications, she has worked with AM in comparison with traditional manufacturing: where it creates real value, where conventional methods remain better, and which technical, economic, and organizational conditions are required for adoption to hold.

The work has never been about a printer in isolation. It has involved materials, process stability, powder handling, downstream processing, qualification pressure, economics, operator workflows, customer readiness, and support load. Metal AM is highly process-sensitive: differences in equipment configuration, material, build preparation, process parameters, operation, or downstream workflows can shift reliability, repeatability, and final part quality. Working in metal AM often means rebuilding the rulebook in a structured way: testing, observing, documenting, comparing, and turning uncertain process behavior into repeatable system logic.

The pressure is real. A failed build can mean expensive material loss, downstream scrap, lost machine time, delayed customer milestones, or a support escalation that pulls scarce experts away from development work. Missed technical or logistics deadlines can damage customer confidence and purchase momentum. Qualification pressure limits how casually a process can be changed. In live environments, unclear instructions or misunderstood failure modes become system-level consequences.

Her additive-manufacturing experience follows an unusually coherent progression through the industrialization arc of a newer manufacturing technology: evaluation, adoption, execution, stabilization, scaling, and enablement.

* **Volvo Cars:** evaluating where additive manufacturing actually fits, and where conventional manufacturing remains better.

* **GE Additive:** supported adoption decisions by helping customers reason through high-investment industrial metal AM, benchmark design, feasibility, logistics, milestone credibility, and ROI-backed decision-making. She translated technically complex customer requirements into benchmark workflows, feasibility framing, and ROI-backed adoption decisions for industrial EBM systems. The work involved materials such as Ti-6Al-4V, cobalt-chromium, Inconel 718, titanium aluminides, copper, and other alloys, often tied to aerospace and medical applications with demanding technical constraints.

* **Markforged:** worked in execution, stabilization, troubleshooting, scaling, and enablement of live binder-jetting systems across customer onboarding, training, support, and ISO 9001 in-house production. She worked extensively with troubleshooting systems, process monitoring, defect diagnostics, support enablement, customer onboarding, and customer training architecture as head of training for binder jetting. Her root-cause analysis work covered both ISO 9001 in-house production issues and customer issues, combining part inspection, defect morphology, process-stage reasoning, logs, operator input, and engineering support.

A defining aspect of this experience is not only technical depth, but systems integration: connecting materials, manufacturing, economics, software-enabled workflows, operators, training, and organizational adoption into systems that are easier to understand, troubleshoot, and scale.
