## Computational Engineer

Complex engineering environments depend on hidden rules: process dependencies, material constraints, workflow sequences, configuration logic, and operational trade-offs that determine whether technical work executes reliably or breaks down under real-world conditions. These rules are often understood tacitly by experts but remain difficult for others to see, apply, or scale consistently.

Terese captures this hidden logic and translates it into practical systems others can reliably use.

Her work centers on reconstructing complex engineering dependencies and encoding them into models, semi-automated workflows, configuration tools, and structured logic frameworks that make technical reasoning explicit, repeatable, and easier to execute. By converting tacit system behavior into operational tools, she reduces ambiguity, removes repetitive expert-dependent work, and creates more reliable pathways for technical and commercial execution.

She treats modeling and automation as active engineering design rather than passive documentation. Her approach follows a consistent pattern:

* **Map:** Identify the variables, constraints, and dependency structures governing system behavior.
* **Formalize:** Reconstruct their logical, mathematical, or procedural relationships.
* **Automate:** Encode that logic into practical systems—templates, scripts, models, workflows, and decision-support tools that others can execute consistently.

This capability has taken multiple forms across industrial environments.

* **At GE Additive,** she co-developed and adapted commercially deployed Excel-based logic systems that translated process dependencies, equipment constraints, consumable dynamics, and production-cycle behavior into predictive configuration and evaluation tools. These systems supported customer feasibility assessment, system configuration, and purchasing decisions under real commercial pressure, while continuously anchoring theoretical assumptions against live production behavior.

* **At Markforged,** she encoded multi-stage manufacturing dependencies into structured configuration tools and operational logic frameworks that reduced ambiguity across deployment planning, consumable management, workflow coordination, and technical support. This created shared execution logic commercial and technical teams could operate from consistently.

* **At Volvo Cars,** she built multi-variable evaluation frameworks that mapped technical feasibility, manufacturing suitability, and implementation constraints directly to make-versus-buy and business-case logic for advanced manufacturing adoption.

Alongside these larger systems, she consistently builds targeted automation to eliminate repetitive technical work. This has included scripting geometry-driven preparation workflows, automating repetitive CAD logic such as nesting and stacking initialization, and designing semi-automated internal tooling that converts manual expert reasoning into reliable execution flows.

Across these environments, the underlying methodology remains consistent: capture hidden engineering logic, formalize its governing relationships, and encode that structure into tools and automation that make complex systems easier to reason about, execute, and scale.

The result is not better documentation. It is engineering logic made operational.


















To a layman, I’d describe it as the engineering equivalent of **finding the hidden rules that make a complicated system work — and then turning those rules into something clear, reliable, and reusable.**

The intrinsic motivation usually comes from a few overlapping satisfactions:

### 1. Turning confusion into clarity

Many complex systems feel messy on the surface.

Things appear to “just happen” because:

* too many variables interact
* people rely on habit or tacit experience
* no one can fully explain why one outcome works and another fails

For an engineer drawn to this kind of work, there is deep satisfaction in untangling that mess and discovering:

**“Ah — these are the actual rules driving the behavior.”**

It’s similar to solving a puzzle where the pieces were never labeled.

---

### 2. Making invisible logic visible

A lot of expert work depends on intuition built through experience.

Someone “just knows” what setting to choose, what sequence to follow, or what warning signs matter.

That works — until that person is unavailable.

The satisfying challenge is extracting that invisible know-how and turning it into something explicit:

* a tool
* a model
* a workflow
* an automated sequence

It’s the pleasure of turning tacit intuition into shared structure.

---

### 3. Building elegant leverage

This is a big one.

There is a specific kind of engineering satisfaction in solving a problem once in a way that prevents hundreds of future problems.

Instead of repeatedly answering the same question or fixing the same issue manually, you build something that makes the right outcome happen more naturally.

It’s the difference between:

**carrying buckets of water every day**

versus

**building plumbing**

People drawn to this work find the second deeply satisfying.

---

### 4. Reducing unnecessary friction

A lot of technical environments contain avoidable effort:

* repetitive manual steps
* ambiguity
* avoidable rework
* constant clarification loops
* dependence on remembering unwritten rules

For someone wired like this, that friction feels almost physically noticeable.

There is genuine intrinsic reward in seeing:

> “This doesn’t need to be this messy.”

…and then redesigning it into something cleaner.

It’s a form of optimization that feels almost aesthetic.

---

### 5. Creating systems that keep working without you

This is often the deepest satisfaction.

You build something, step away, and it still works.

People can use it.
Decisions become clearer.
Work flows better.
The system behaves more intelligently because the logic has been embedded into it.

That creates a strong sense of durable impact.

**It’s the satisfaction of spotting hidden patterns inside complex technical systems, understanding the rules that govern them, and then designing tools or workflows that make those systems easier for everyone else to use correctly.**
